Advanced Musical Terms

  • Allegory — in music, this refers to the application of symbolic or hidden meanings through musical forms, themes, or instruments.
  • Alteration — changing the pitch of a note by a half step (or whole step) using sharps or flats.
  • Appreggiatura — a system of instructions on finger usage for playing a musical instrument, especially string and keyboard instruments.
  • Arpeggio — performing the notes of a chord in sequence rather than simultaneously.
  • Cadenza — a concluding turn in a musical piece that creates a sense of resolution; often used at the end of a phrase or work.
  • Counterpoint — a technique of combining two or more independent melodies that sound simultaneously.
  • Dodecaphony — a compositional technique that uses all 12 tones of the chromatic scale equally, without privileging any of them.
  • Glissando — a smooth movement from one note to another that creates a sliding sound.
  • Tritone — an interval that spans three whole tones, or 6 semitones; usually considered a "dissonant" or unstable interval.
  • Retardation — a musical device where a note that would typically disappear or change is delayed to create a sense of tension.
  • Temperament — a system for tuning musical instruments that involves uneven distribution of intervals to achieve harmony in a particular key (the most well-known system is equal temperament).
  • Sequence — the repetition of a musical motif or phrase at a different pitch, often within a single work.
  • Syncopation — the shifting of emphasis to a weak beat of a measure or pulse, creating the effect of an unexpected rhythmic shift.
  • Polyrhythm — the simultaneous use of different rhythmic patterns or meters within a single musical piece.
  • Formant — resonant frequencies that determine the timbre of sound and the characteristic quality of a voice or instrument.
